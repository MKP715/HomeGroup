Section 2

Historical Evolution

⏰ 15 minutes

From living fellowship to structured home groups — the term "home group" does not appear in AA's earliest literature

1935 – 1939
The Smith Home Era — The Prototype

Dr. Bob and Anne Smith's residence at 855 Ardmore Avenue, Akron functioned as meeting house, laboratory of recovery, and refuge. Bill W. lived there several months. Meetings included "quiet time, prayer, and bible readings" with Anne conducting morning readings from the Book of James. Members hospitalized prospects before allowing them to join.

Dr. Bob and the Good Oldtimers, p. 101

May 1939
First Group Named "Alcoholics Anonymous"

Cleveland, Ohio. Clarence S. wrote to Bill W. describing their approach: "Not too much emphasis on spiritual business at meetings. Have discussions after meetings of any business or questions arising. Plenty of fellowship all the time."

Dr. Bob and the Good Oldtimers, p. 167

1940
The Akron Manual — Earliest "Home Group" Language

Written and distributed by "Dr. Bob's Home Group, Akron AA group #1." It stated: "Meetings provide a means for an exchange of ideas, the renewing of fellowships... a sense of security" and "Remember that attendance at meetings is one of the most important requisites of remaining sober."

1946 – 1950
The Twelve Traditions Developed

Bill W. wrote the Traditions (first published in the Grapevine, April 1946), codifying decades of group experience. These became the spiritual principles governing how groups — including home groups — relate to each other and to AA as a whole.

Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions (1953)

1993
First Grapevine Book: "The Home Group"

The concept formally crystallized with this publication collecting member stories about their home group experiences. This marked the first dedicated AA publication on the topic.

September 2000
"The Home Group" Becomes Grapevine Department

Due to overwhelming reader response, "The Home Group" became a permanent dedicated department in AA Grapevine magazine.

Present Day
30th Anniversary Edition — Including Virtual Meetings

"The Home Group: Heartbeat of AA" now includes content on virtual meetings, demonstrating the concept continues to evolve while its core purpose remains unchanged.

Grapevine publication (not pamphlet P-88 as sometimes misidentified)

The Home Group & Sponsorship Connection

⏰ 10 minutes

A symbiotic relationship — each strengthens the other

The pamphlet "Questions and Answers on Sponsorship" (P-15) explicitly connects home group and sponsorship, describing sponsorship as "the bridge enabling the new person to meet other alcoholics — in a home group and other groups visited."

🎯
Guiding Newcomers
P-15 states a sponsor:
  • "Encourages, and if necessary, helps the newcomer to attend a variety of A.A. meetings"
  • "Introduces the newcomer to other members"
  • "Urges the newcomer to join in group activities as soon as possible"

P-15, pp. 7–8, 12

🔧
Service Sponsorship
P-15 recommends: "The service sponsor begins by encouraging the member to become active in their home group — coffee, literature, cleanup, attending business or intergroup meetings, etc."

Service sponsors should: "Be familiar with the AA Service Manual, Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions, Circles of Love and Service, and The AA Home Group pamphlet."

P-15, pp. 25–26

Building Sponsorship Programs Within the Group

"Active sponsorship programs within a group remind all members of the group's primary purpose. They serve to unite a group and keep it mindful of First Things First."

— P-15, pp. 23–25

Suggested practices: regular beginners meetings, member assignments to greet newcomers, and announcements offering temporary sponsors.

"...he finds he cannot keep this priceless gift unless he carries the A.A. message... the moment this Twelfth Step work forms a group, another discovery is made — that most individuals cannot recover unless there is a group."

— Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions, p. 130

Real-World Scenarios

🎭 Scenario: The Newcomer at the Door
Someone walks into your home group for the very first time. They're shaking. They don't know anyone. They sit in the back. The meeting starts. The meeting ends. Nobody talks to them. They leave. They never come back.

Now picture the alternative: A greeter meets them. Introduces them to three people. Hands them a phone list. After the meeting, two members invite them for coffee. One offers to be a temporary sponsor.

Which scenario reflects our primary purpose?

P-15 says service sponsorship begins in the home group with simple things: coffee, literature, cleanup, attending business meetings.

Section 8

The Twelve Traditions as Home Group Guidance

⏰ 15 minutes

Five Traditions that most directly shape home group life — click each to expand

1 Unity — Common Welfare Comes First

"Each member of Alcoholics Anonymous is but a small part of a great whole. A.A. must continue to live or most of us will surely die. Hence our common welfare comes first. But individual welfare follows close afterward."

— Tradition One (Long Form), 12&12, p. 189

The 12&12 elaborates (p. 129): "Without unity, the heart of A.A. would cease to beat; our world arteries would no longer carry the life-giving grace of God; His gift to us would be spent aimlessly."

2 Group Conscience — One Ultimate Authority

"For our group purpose there is but one ultimate authority — a loving God as He may express Himself in our group conscience. Our leaders are but trusted servants; they do not govern."

— Tradition Two

Bill W. explained in AA Comes of Age (p. 99): "Harder still to accept was the now proven fact that the conscience of the group, when properly informed of the facts and issues and principles involved, was often wiser than any leader, self-appointed or not."

3 Membership — The Only Requirement

"The only requirement for A.A. membership is a desire to stop drinking."

— Tradition Three

The long form adds (12&12, p. 139): "Any two or three alcoholics gathered together for sobriety may call themselves an A.A. group, provided that, as a group, they have no other affiliation."

This means: "You are an A.A. member if you say so. You can declare yourself in; nobody can keep you out."

4 Autonomy — Each Group Is Self-Governing

"Each group should be autonomous except in matters affecting other groups or A.A. as a whole."

— Tradition Four

This allows each home group to develop its own character — meeting format, literature used, procedures followed — while maintaining connection to AA's principles. No two home groups are alike, and that is by design.

5 Primary Purpose — Carry the Message

"Each group has but one primary purpose — to carry its message to the alcoholic who still suffers."

— Tradition Five, 12&12, p. 150

The 12&12 explains: "'Shoemaker, stick to thy last!'...better do one thing supremely well than many badly. That is the central theme of this Tradition."

Section 9

Participating in Informed Group Conscience

⏰ 10 minutes

The process that distinguishes AA from other organizations

"The 'group conscience' is the collective conscience of the group membership and thus represents substantial unanimity on an issue before definitive action is taken. This is achieved by the group members through the sharing of full information, individual points of view, and the practice of A.A. principles. To be fully 'informed' requires a willingness to listen to minority opinions with an open mind."

— P-16 "The A.A. Group," pp. 28–30

Essential Elements of the Process

🔍 Information Gathering ▼

All pertinent facts are presented before discussion begins. This may include reading relevant Traditions, reviewing precedents from other groups, or consulting Conference-approved literature.

💬 Full Discussion ▼

All views are heard, including minority opinions. Every home group member who wishes to speak has the opportunity. No voice is silenced.

⏳ Deliberation Time ▼

On sensitive issues, groups work slowly. There is no rush. Some groups table important issues for a month to allow reflection and prayer.

⚖ Avoiding Dominant Voices ▼

Membership remains wary of dominating personalities. Tradition Two reminds us that leaders are trusted servants — they do not govern.

✅ Substantial Unanimity ▼

Results rest on more than a simple "yes" or "no" count. Substantial unanimity (typically two-thirds) ensures that the group truly speaks with one voice.

When Even a Co-Founder Was Overruled

"'Bill, haven't you often said right here in this meeting that sometimes the good is the enemy of the best? Well, this is a plain case of it. You can't do this thing to us!' So spoke the group conscience. The group was right and I was wrong... Here was the true voice, welling up out of my friends. I listened and — thank God — I obeyed."

— Bill W., AA Comes of Age, p. 101

Business meetings typically occur monthly or quarterly, with voting restricted to those who identify the group as their home group. Each member has one vote — the foundation of AA's democratic character.

Section 11

Common Challenges & How Groups Address Them

⏰ 10 minutes

Every home group faces recurring tests of the Traditions

🗣
Dominant Personalities
Threatens Tradition Two's servant leadership. The 12&12 teaches: group conscience, properly informed, proves wiser than any individual.

Solution: Ensure all voices are heard. Rotate leadership positions. Practice "principles before personalities."

Tradition 2

🔒
Cliques & Exclusivity
Contradicts Tradition Three's open membership. P-16 reminds: "We also learn firsthand, through the group's workings, how to place 'principles before personalities' in the interest of carrying the A.A. message."

Solution: Assign greeters. Consciously include newcomers in fellowship.

Tradition 3

📰
Outside Issues
Violates Traditions Six (no endorsement) and Ten (no opinion on outside issues). Groups must maintain focus on alcoholism recovery.

Solution: Gently redirect. Read Tradition Ten at business meetings. Stay focused on primary purpose.

Traditions 6 & 10

💰
Financial Challenges
Tests Tradition Seven (self-supporting through own contributions). Transparency and education are essential.

Solution: Regular financial reports. Maintain prudent reserves. Educate members on service structure funding needs.

Tradition 7

😴
Apathy in Service
Threatens group sustainability when few members participate. Groups address this by demonstrating how service strengthens recovery and creating welcoming entry points for new service commitment. Even making coffee is spiritually significant.

Scenario Bank for Discussion

⏰ Use as needed for roundtables

Real-world situations for group discussion — click each to expand

🎭 The Relapsing Member ▼

A home group member with 2 years of sobriety relapses. They come back to the meeting, visibly ashamed. Some members welcome them warmly. Others seem uncomfortable. One old-timer says at the business meeting, "Maybe we need to be more careful about who we let share."

Discussion: What Traditions apply? (3, 1, 5) What is the group's responsibility? What would your home group do?

🎭 The Non-Alcoholic Attendee ▼

Someone attends your open home group regularly. They share that they're not an alcoholic but have a family member who is. They want to "join" the group. At the business meeting, a member asks whether this person should be allowed to vote.

Discussion: What does Tradition Three say? What's the difference between an open and closed meeting? Can this person be a "member"?

🎭 The Money Question ▼

Your home group has accumulated $3,000 in its treasury. Some members want to donate it all to intergroup. Others say keep a larger prudent reserve. The treasurer suggests splitting it between the district, NETA 65, and GSO.

Discussion: What does Tradition Seven say? What does the AA Service Manual suggest about fund flow? What is a "prudent reserve"? How does your group currently handle excess funds?

🎭 The Format War ▼

Half the home group wants to switch from a speaker meeting to a Big Book study. The other half loves the current format. The debate has been going on for three business meetings. Attendance is dropping because people are frustrated.

Discussion: How does an informed group conscience handle this? What does Tradition Four allow? Could the group compromise? What if the group splits — is that failure or growth?

🎭 The Online Question ▼

Since the pandemic, your home group has offered a hybrid option (in-person + Zoom). Some members think Zoom should be permanent. Others feel it reduces the quality of fellowship and that "real AA" is in person only.

Discussion: What does Tradition Four say about group autonomy? How do you balance accessibility with the value of in-person fellowship? What does the 30th Anniversary "Home Group: Heartbeat" say about virtual meetings?

🎭 The Anonymity Break ▼

A home group member posts a photo from the meeting on social media, tagging other members by name. Some members are upset. Others say, "It's just social media, relax."

Discussion: What do Traditions 11 and 12 say about anonymity? What is the difference between personal anonymity and breaking others' anonymity? How should the group address this?

🎭 The Crosstalk Debate ▼

Your home group has always had a "no crosstalk" guideline. A newer member says this feels cold and clinical — they want people to respond to each other, ask follow-up questions, and have real dialogue. An old-timer says the no-crosstalk rule protects vulnerable members from unsolicited advice.

Discussion: Is "no crosstalk" a Tradition or a group guideline? How does Tradition Four apply? How do you balance safety with genuine connection?

The Foundation: Why Does Anonymity Exist?

⏰ 10 minutes

"Anonymity is the greatest protection our Society can ever have."

— Dr. Bob, AA Co-Founder (AA Comes of Age, p. 139)

Three Fundamental Reasons for Anonymity

👤
1. Personal Protection
In 1935, alcoholism was considered a moral failing, not a disease. Anonymity protected members from job loss, social ostracism, and family shame. This protection remains vital today.
🏛️
2. Organizational Protection
AA feared becoming a cult of personality. If individual members became famous spokespeople, their relapses or misconduct could destroy public trust in AA itself.
✨
3. Spiritual Practice
Anonymity is a daily discipline in humility. It reminds us that principles come before personalities — the message matters more than the messenger.

What Anonymity Is NOT

❌ Anonymity Is NOT Secrecy Within the Fellowship ▼

We use first names at meetings not to hide our identities, but to practice humility and equality. Within AA, we share openly. The Big Book itself contains full stories with real details — anonymity protects us outside the rooms, at the level of press, radio, films, and public media.

Reference: "Anonymity," The Language of the Heart, pp. 68-69

❌ Anonymity Does NOT Mean You Can't Tell Anyone ▼

Many members are open about their AA membership with family, friends, employers, and doctors. Anonymity is about public media — not private conversations. You can be public about your own membership; you cannot be public about someone else's membership.

Reference: "Understanding Anonymity," AA Grapevine, June 1981

❌ Anonymity Is NOT a Loophole for Bad Behavior ▼

Some members mistakenly believe anonymity protects them from accountability within the fellowship. Wrong. If someone harms another member, the group conscience can address it. Anonymity protects AA's public reputation, not individual misconduct within the fellowship.

Reference: P-16 "The A.A. Group," p. 28

Anonymity in Your Home Group

How does anonymity show up in your home group every week?

  • ✓ No last names — Everyone is equal, whether they're a doctor or a day laborer
  • ✓ No crosstalk — The message matters more than rebutting personalities
  • ✓ Rotation of service positions — No one becomes "the face" of the group
  • ✓ Conference-approved literature only — Principles, not personal interpretations
  • ✓ "Who you see here, what you hear here..." — Protecting each other's stories

"The spiritual substance of anonymity is sacrifice. Because A.A.'s Twelve Traditions repeatedly ask us to give up personal ambitions and desires for the common good, we realize that the sacrificial spirit — well symbolized by anonymity — is the foundation of them all."

Anonymity • Part 2

Historical Evolution of Anonymity in AA

⏰ 10 minutes

Anonymity has evolved significantly since 1935. Understanding this history helps us appreciate why the Traditions are written as they are — and why our founders were so adamant about protecting it.

1935–1939
The Early Days: Safety from Stigma

In the 1930s, alcoholism was widely considered a moral failing, not a disease. Alcoholics were fired from jobs, institutionalized, and socially shunned. Anonymity was essential for survival.

The first AA groups operated in complete secrecy. Members used only first names, even with each other. Public admission of alcoholism could destroy careers and families.

Reference: Alcoholics Anonymous Comes of Age, pp. 135-136

1939–1941
The Cleveland Plain Dealer Break

In October 1939, the Cleveland Plain Dealer published a series of articles about AA. One member, Elgie R., gave his full name and was photographed. Within a year, Elgie relapsed — and the newspaper ran another story: "Former AA Member Dies Drunk."

This devastated Cleveland AA's credibility. The fellowship nearly collapsed. Bill W. later called this incident the "greatest threat to AA's future we ever faced."

Reference: Pass It On, pp. 282-283

1941
The Saturday Evening Post (Jack Alexander Article)

On March 1, 1941, the Saturday Evening Post published "Alcoholics Anonymous: Freed Slaves of Drink, Now They Free Others" by Jack Alexander. This article quadrupled AA membership overnight — from 2,000 to 8,000 members within a year.

Critically, Alexander respected anonymity. No full names were used. No faces were photographed. The article promoted AA without promoting individuals. This was the model for all future public relations.

Dallas Connection: Esther E.'s husband read the Jack Alexander article, gave her an ultimatum, and she contacted the General Service Office — directly leading to Dallas AA's founding on April 2, 1943. The article's respectful approach to anonymity made it effective and safe.

Reference: AA Comes of Age, pp. 176-178

1944
Bill W.'s Anonymity Break (Filming of "The Big World")

In 1944, a promotional film titled "The Big World" was planned. Bill W. agreed to appear on camera and reveal his full identity to promote AA. Dr. Bob vehemently opposed this. After intense debate, Bill backed down — but not before the fellowship was shaken.

Bill later wrote: "I had been ready to place AA in serious jeopardy for my own sake. It was then I began to understand that anonymity was humility at the level of Tradition."

Reference: Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions, p. 187

1946
Codification: The Twelve Traditions Drafted

After a decade of painful lessons, Bill W. drafted the Twelve Traditions in 1946. Two Traditions focus specifically on anonymity:

  • Tradition Eleven: Public relations policy — never promote ourselves at the level of press, radio, and films
  • Tradition Twelve: Anonymity as the spiritual foundation of all Traditions — principles before personalities

These weren't abstract ideals. They were lessons written in blood — relapses, broken trust, damaged groups.

Reference: AA Comes of Age, pp. 137-141

1955–1971
Dr. Bob and Bill's Final Years

Dr. Bob died in 1950 maintaining complete anonymity. He never sought recognition and rarely spoke outside Ohio. Bill W., despite his earlier struggles, also maintained anonymity at the public level. Neither founder ever revealed their full names in press, radio, or film.

Bill passed away in 1971. His New York Times obituary respected his anonymity — calling him "Bill W., founder of Alcoholics Anonymous." Even in death, the principle held.

Reference: Dr. Bob and the Good Oldtimers, pp. 310-311; Pass It On, pp. 402-403

1955–Present
Modern Challenges: The Digital Age

The internet, social media, and smartphones have created unprecedented anonymity challenges:

  • Members posting meeting photos on Facebook or Instagram
  • YouTube channels with "My AA Story" videos using full names
  • Public figures disclosing their AA membership on talk shows
  • Zoom meetings recorded or screenshotted without consent

The principle of anonymity hasn't changed. The application requires constant vigilance.

Reference: "Anonymity Online," pamphlet, GSO 2018

The Two Levels of Anonymity

⏰ 15 minutes

The most common source of confusion about anonymity comes from failing to understand that there are two distinct levels. Clarifying this distinction will resolve 90% of anonymity questions.

🗣️
Personal Level: Your Choice

At the personal level, anonymity is optional. You decide who knows about your AA membership in your private life.

You MAY:

  • Tell your family, friends, coworkers, or employer
  • Discuss your recovery in private conversations
  • Wear AA-themed clothing or jewelry
  • Identify yourself as an AA member in non-public settings

"Many of us are willing to declare our membership in Alcoholics Anonymous in our ordinary affairs."

— Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions, p. 184

📺
Public Media Level: Protected

At the level of public media, anonymity is required. This protects AA from the consequences of individual relapses or misconduct.

You MAY NOT:

  • Use your full name as an AA member in press, radio, films, TV, or internet
  • Appear in documentaries or news stories identifying yourself as an AA member
  • Post on social media (Facebook, Instagram, TikTok) using your real name and identifying as an AA member
  • Allow your face and full name to be connected to AA membership in any permanent, public record

"We must always maintain personal anonymity at the level of press, radio, and films."

— Tradition Eleven

Why the Distinction Matters

Imagine a well-known actor holds a press conference and declares: "I'm John Doe, and I'm a member of Alcoholics Anonymous. AA saved my life." Six months later, John Doe is arrested for drunk driving. The headlines read: "AA Member John Doe Arrested for DUI — Does AA Work?"

John's relapse is now AA's problem. If he had maintained anonymity, his relapse would have been a personal matter. This is why Tradition Eleven exists: to protect the fellowship from being judged by individual failures.

Real-World Examples

✅ Example 1: Talking to Your Boss ▼

Scenario: You need to leave work early on Tuesdays for your home group meeting. You tell your boss: "I attend Alcoholics Anonymous meetings. I need Tuesday afternoons off."

Is this an anonymity break? NO. This is a private, personal conversation. Your boss is not "press, radio, or films." You're making a personal disclosure to facilitate your recovery.

Reference: "Questions & Answers on Sponsorship," p. 28

✅ Example 2: Sharing at an Open AA Meeting ▼

Scenario: A documentary filmmaker attends an open AA meeting and records you sharing. You give your first name only. The filmmaker uses the footage in a film about addiction recovery, showing your face but not your last name.

Is this an anonymity break? GRAY AREA. Technically, open meetings are public. However, most AA groups request that media identify members as "an AA member" without showing faces. Best practice: The group should have voted on allowing filming, and members should consent individually.

Reference: "Understanding Anonymity," pamphlet, p. 12

❌ Example 3: Facebook Post with Full Name ▼

Scenario: You post on Facebook: "Today I'm celebrating 10 years sober in Alcoholics Anonymous! My name is Jane Smith, and I'm so grateful for this program."

Is this an anonymity break? YES. Social media (Facebook, Instagram, Twitter/X, TikTok) is "public media." Even if your profile is set to "Friends Only," posts can be shared, screenshotted, and spread beyond your control. This is a break of Tradition Eleven.

Alternative: Post "Today I'm celebrating 10 years sober in a 12-step program." Or use a pseudonym/anonymous account.

Reference: "Anonymity Online," GSO pamphlet

❌ Example 4: Celebrity Disclosing AA Membership on TV ▼

Scenario: A famous musician appears on a late-night talk show and says: "I've been sober for five years thanks to Alcoholics Anonymous."

Is this an anonymity break? YES. Television is explicitly mentioned in Tradition Eleven. While the celebrity's intentions may be good (reducing stigma, inspiring others), they're placing their personal story above AA's principle. If they relapse publicly, it harms AA's reputation.

What they should say instead: "I've been sober for five years thanks to a 12-step program" or "thanks to AA — but I respect the tradition of anonymity and won't say more."

Reference: Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions, pp. 184-187

⚠️ Example 5: Zoom Meetings & Screenshots ▼

Scenario: You attend a Zoom AA meeting. Someone takes a screenshot showing everyone's faces and names, then posts it on Instagram with the caption "My AA family!"

Is this an anonymity break? YES. Even though Zoom is a gray area, AA's guidance is clear: Treat virtual meetings like in-person meetings. No recording. No screenshots. No posting attendees' images online.

Tradition Eleven: Our Public Relations Policy

⏰ 15 minutes

"Our public relations policy is based on attraction rather than promotion; we need always maintain personal anonymity at the level of press, radio, and films."

— Tradition Eleven

Breaking Down Tradition Eleven

Tradition Eleven has two distinct parts. Let's examine each carefully.

🧲
Part 1: Attraction Rather Than Promotion

Attraction means letting our results speak for themselves. We don't advertise, we don't hire spokespeople, we don't seek publicity. Instead, we simply live sober lives — and people notice.

Promotion means actively marketing AA, seeking endorsements, or campaigning for membership. AA does none of this.

"The public needs to understand that we have no membership drives, that we do not solicit members, that we wish our affairs to remain uncluttered by politics and religious disputes."

— Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions, p. 180

🎭
Part 2: Personal Anonymity at Public Level

We maintain personal anonymity (no full names, no faces) at the level of press, radio, films — and today, TV, internet, and social media.

Why? Because individual relapses harm AA's public image. If AA promoted individual success stories with full names and faces, every relapse would become a scandal.

"We must never let any desire for personal prestige or distinction tempt us to break our anonymity before the public in press, radio, television, or films."

— Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions, p. 183

Why "Attraction, Not Promotion"?

📖 Historical Lesson: The Washingtonian Society ▼

In the 1840s, a temperance organization called the Washingtonian Society grew rapidly through promotion. They held massive rallies, recruited celebrity speakers, and published names of reformed drunkards.

Within five years, the Washingtonians collapsed. Why? Their celebrity speakers relapsed publicly. Internal power struggles erupted over who got to speak. They endorsed political candidates and divided the membership.

Bill W. studied the Washingtonians closely. He vowed AA would never repeat their mistakes. That's why we have "attraction, not promotion."

"We have no desire to convince anyone that there is only one way by which faith can be acquired... Whatever your own way may be, you may still be interested in the Washingtonian movement of a century ago."

— Alcoholics Anonymous (Big Book), Appendix II: Spiritual Experience

🎬 Modern Example: Celebrity Anonymity Breaks ▼

When a celebrity publicly identifies as an AA member and later relapses, the media coverage inevitably questions AA's effectiveness. Headlines like "AA Failed Celebrity X" or "Does AA Actually Work?" emerge — even though the celebrity's relapse is an individual matter, not a failure of the program.

If the celebrity had maintained anonymity, their relapse would be a private struggle. But by breaking anonymity, they made their recovery (and relapse) AA's business in the public eye.

This is exactly what Tradition Eleven protects against.

🏠 How Your Home Group Practices Attraction ▼

Your home group doesn't need to advertise. Instead, it practices attraction by:

  • Showing up consistently — The meeting is there every week, rain or shine
  • Being welcoming — Greeting newcomers, offering phone numbers, making coffee
  • Carrying the message — Speaking at treatment centers, jails, and hospitals (but always using first names only)
  • Living sober — Members' transformed lives inspire others without a single word of promotion

"Our growth is natural — it's one drunk talking to another. That's attraction. We don't need to hire a PR firm or put up billboards. The program sells itself."

Applying Tradition Eleven Today

Tradition Eleven was written in 1946, before television, the internet, or social media existed. How do we apply it today?

Press & Print Media

Original intent: Newspapers, magazines

Modern application: Online news articles, blogs, Medium posts

Rule: No full names, no faces connected to AA membership

Radio & Films

Original intent: Broadcast radio, movie theaters

Modern application: Podcasts, YouTube, streaming documentaries, TikTok videos

Rule: No full names, no faces connected to AA membership

Social Media (New Territory)

What it is: Facebook, Instagram, Twitter/X, TikTok, LinkedIn

AA's position: Social media is "public media" — Tradition Eleven applies

Rule: Don't post "I'm [Full Name] and I'm in AA" on public or semi-public accounts

Common Questions About Tradition Eleven

Q: Can I share my AA story on my personal blog or podcast? ▼

A: If you use your full name and identify yourself as an AA member, this is a break of Tradition Eleven. Blogs and podcasts are public media.

Alternative: Use a pseudonym, or say "I attend a 12-step program" instead of "Alcoholics Anonymous."

Many recovery advocates run successful blogs and podcasts without breaking anonymity by using their first name only or a pen name.

Q: What if a news reporter wants to interview me about AA? ▼

A: You can do the interview if you maintain anonymity. Give your first name only, don't show your face, or have your face obscured (blurred, silhouetted, filmed from behind).

Best practice: Refer the reporter to AA's Public Information office. They have trained media liaisons who know how to speak about AA without breaking anonymity.

Reference: "Understanding Anonymity," pamphlet, p. 15

Q: Can my home group have a Facebook page or Instagram account? ▼

A: Yes, but with caution. Many home groups have social media pages to post meeting times, events, and recovery quotes. However:

  • Never post photos showing members' faces without explicit consent
  • Never tag individual members or use their full names
  • Never post screenshots of private group chats or Zoom meetings
  • Moderate comments to prevent members from "outing" themselves or others

Safest approach: Post only meeting info, AA-approved literature quotes, and generic recovery images (no faces).

Reference: "Anonymity Online," GSO bulletin

Tradition Twelve: The Spiritual Foundation

⏰ 15 minutes

"Anonymity is the spiritual foundation of all our Traditions, ever reminding us to place principles before personalities."

— Tradition Twelve

Why Tradition Twelve Is the "Crown" of the Traditions

Bill W. deliberately made anonymity the final Tradition because it undergirds all the others. Without anonymity, the other 11 Traditions would eventually fail. Let's see how.

Traditions 1-3: Unity

Tradition 1: Common welfare comes first — anonymity ensures no individual is more important than the group.

Tradition 2: One ultimate authority (God as expressed in group conscience) — anonymity prevents charismatic leaders from dominating.

Tradition 3: The only requirement is a desire to stop drinking — anonymity ensures everyone is equal, no hierarchy based on status.

Traditions 4-6: Autonomy & Singleness of Purpose

Traditions 4-5: Each group is autonomous, with one primary purpose — anonymity prevents outside interests or personal agendas from infiltrating.

Tradition 6: Never endorse or lend the AA name — anonymity ensures we can't be used by individuals for personal gain.

Traditions 7-11: Financial Independence & Humility

Tradition 7: Self-supporting — anonymity prevents wealthy members from buying influence.

Traditions 8-10: Non-professional, no property or management — anonymity ensures service is about humility, not prestige.

Tradition 11: Attraction, not promotion — anonymity prevents cult of personality.

Principles Before Personalities: What Does It Mean?

"Principles before personalities" is the most quoted phrase in AA — and the most misunderstood. It doesn't mean we ignore individual needs or suppress personality. It means that when personalities clash with principles, principles win.

Example 1: The Long-Winded Speaker ▼

Scenario: A member regularly speaks for 10-15 minutes at meetings, leaving little time for others. Some members love his shares; others feel silenced.

Personality approach: "John is a great speaker and everyone loves him. Let him talk."

Principle approach: "Our principle is that everyone gets a chance to share (Tradition 1: common welfare). We need to enforce a 3-5 minute sharing limit — for everyone, including John."

Principle wins. John's personality doesn't override the group's unity.

Example 2: The Wealthy Donor ▼

Scenario: A wealthy member donates $500/month to the group, far exceeding the $5,000 annual contribution limit suggested by Tradition Seven. He begins suggesting how the money should be spent.

Personality approach: "He's so generous! We should listen to his ideas since he supports us."

Principle approach: "We appreciate your generosity, but Tradition Seven asks that no member contribute more than the limit. Your voice in group conscience is equal to every other member's — one person, one vote."

Principle wins. Wealth doesn't buy influence.

Reference: Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions, p. 160

Example 3: The Popular Group Leader ▼

Scenario: Your home group's secretary has served for five years. She's beloved, organized, and the "face" of the group. Some members say, "We could never replace her."

Personality approach: "She's irreplaceable. Let her stay as secretary forever."

Principle approach: "Rotation of service is a core principle (Tradition Two: trusted servants, not governors). After her term ends, we rotate to a new secretary. This ensures no one becomes indispensable and prevents burnout."

Principle wins. Service positions rotate, even when the current servant is excellent.

Reference: The A.A. Service Manual, p. S19

Example 4: The Celebrity Member ▼

Scenario: A local news anchor joins your home group. Members start bringing friends just to meet him. He's asked to speak at events more than other members.

Personality approach: "Having a celebrity helps us attract newcomers. Let's feature him."

Principle approach: "In AA, there are no celebrities. He's a member like everyone else. We won't promote him or treat him differently. If he wants to speak, he goes on the speaker rotation like anyone else."

Principle wins. Fame doesn't grant privilege in AA.

The Spiritual Discipline of Anonymity

Bill W. called anonymity a spiritual discipline — not a rule, but a practice that shapes our character. How?

🙏
Humility

Anonymity teaches us to serve without seeking credit. In your home group, you set up chairs, make coffee, greet newcomers — and no one applauds. That's the point. Service is its own reward.

"True humility and an open mind can lead us to faith, and every A.A. meeting is an assurance that God will restore us to sanity if we rightly relate ourselves to Him."

— Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions, p. 33

⚖️
Equality

Anonymity reminds us that we're all equal in AA. The doctor and the dishwasher, the CEO and the unemployed — we're all "just another bozo on the bus," as the saying goes. No one's recovery is more valuable than another's.

"At the level of Tradition, therefore, we are all equal in the sight of God and man."

— Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions, p. 184

🛡️
Protection from Ego

Anonymity protects us from our own egos. If we become "known" as AA members, we risk relapse driven by pride, fear of letting others down, or pressure to be perfect. Anonymity frees us to be human.

"The spiritual substance of anonymity is sacrifice. Because A.A.'s Twelve Traditions repeatedly ask us to give up personal ambitions and desires for the common good..."

— The Language of the Heart, p. 71

🤝
Unity

Anonymity prevents factions, hierarchies, and personality cults. When no one is more important than anyone else, we can focus on our shared mission: carrying the message to the alcoholic who still suffers.

"Each member of Alcoholics Anonymous is but a small part of a great whole. Therefore, in the course of our lifetime as A.A. members, we find it wise to subordinate personal ambition to the common welfare."

— Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions, p. 130

Anonymity vs. Confidentiality: Know the Difference

⏰ 10 minutes

One of the most common sources of confusion in AA is the difference between anonymity and confidentiality. They're related but distinct concepts — and understanding the difference is crucial for your home group's safety.

🎭
Anonymity

Definition: Not revealing your own full name and face as an AA member at the level of public media (press, radio, films, TV, internet).

Scope: Your own identity

Level: Public media (Tradition Eleven)

Optional? At the personal level, yes. At the public media level, no.

Example: "I'm choosing not to tell my coworkers I'm in AA" (personal anonymity), or "I won't appear on TV using my full name as an AA member" (public anonymity).

🔒
Confidentiality

Definition: Not revealing someone else's presence at AA, their story, or any identifying details they shared — ever, anywhere, to anyone.

Scope: Other members' identities and stories

Level: Everywhere — meetings, personal conversations, online, always

Optional? NEVER. Confidentiality is absolute.

Example: "I saw my neighbor at an AA meeting. I will never mention this to anyone unless they bring it up first."

Why Confidentiality Is Absolute

Without confidentiality, AA cannot function. Newcomers must feel safe to share their darkest secrets, their relapses, their fears. If they worry that what they say will be repeated outside the room, they won't share honestly — and honest sharing is the lifeblood of recovery.

Scenario 1: "I Saw My Boss at a Meeting" ▼

What happened: You attend your home group meeting and see your boss there. You're shocked — you had no idea they were in recovery.

❌ WRONG response: The next day at work, you say, "Hey, I didn't know you were in AA! I go to meetings too. We should carpool!"

✅ CORRECT response: You say nothing. You wait for your boss to bring it up. If they never mention it, you never mention it. Even if you're both clearly aware of each other's presence at meetings, you let them decide if/when to acknowledge it outside the room.

Why? Because they may not be ready to be "out" as an AA member at work. Respecting their confidentiality protects their job, their privacy, and their dignity.

Scenario 2: "Can I Tell My Spouse What Someone Shared?" ▼

What happened: At your home group, a member shares a shocking story about relapsing and crashing their car. You're emotionally affected and want to process it with your spouse at home.

❌ WRONG response: "Honey, you won't believe what John shared at the meeting tonight. He got drunk and crashed his car into a tree..."

✅ CORRECT response: "Someone at the meeting tonight shared a really intense relapse story. It affected me deeply. I can't tell you the details, but I'm processing it." If you need to debrief, call your sponsor or another AA member — not someone outside the fellowship.

Why? "What you hear here, stays here." Even your spouse doesn't get to know the details of someone else's story unless that person chooses to tell them directly.

Reference: "Understanding Anonymity," pamphlet, p. 18

Scenario 3: "A Celebrity Came to My Meeting" ▼

What happened: A famous actor attends your home group meeting. You're excited and want to tell your friends.

❌ WRONG response: Post on social media: "OMG, [Celebrity Name] was at my AA meeting tonight! So cool to see them working a program!"

✅ CORRECT response: You tell no one. Not your friends, not your family, not social media. Even if the celebrity has publicly disclosed their AA membership, you do not confirm their attendance at your specific meeting.

Why? Because confirming someone's presence at a meeting is a confidentiality breach — even if they're a public figure. They came to your meeting seeking anonymity and safety, not publicity.

Scenario 4: "I Saw Someone From My Meeting Drinking at a Bar" ▼

What happened: You see a member of your home group drinking at a bar. You're concerned about their relapse.

❌ WRONG response: Tell other members at the next meeting: "Hey, did you know Sarah relapsed? I saw her drinking at a bar on Friday."

✅ CORRECT response: If you're close with Sarah, reach out privately: "I saw you the other night. I'm here if you want to talk." If you're not close, do nothing — or mention it to her sponsor if you have their contact and it's an emergency. But do NOT gossip about it at meetings.

Why? Relapse is a personal matter. Broadcasting it to the group is a breach of confidentiality and could shame Sarah away from returning when she's ready to come back.

Scenario 5: "Can I Use Someone's Story in a Lead?" ▼

What happened: You're asked to speak (give a lead) at another group. You want to share a powerful story you heard in your home group from another member.

❌ WRONG response: "A guy in my home group, Bob, shared this incredible story about hitting rock bottom. He said..."

✅ CORRECT response: Either (1) ask Bob's permission first: "I'm speaking at another group. Would it be okay if I shared your story (anonymously, no names)?" or (2) tell the story in a completely anonymized way: "I once heard someone share about..." with no identifying details.

Best practice: Share your own story, not someone else's.

"Who You See Here, What You Hear Here, When You Leave Here, Let It Stay Here"

This phrase, often recited at the end of meetings, is the essence of confidentiality. It means:

  • Who you see here: Don't reveal anyone's attendance at AA meetings
  • What you hear here: Don't repeat anyone else's shares or stories
  • Let it stay here: The room is a sacred, confidential space

How Your Home Group Can Reinforce Confidentiality

Include It in the Format

Many groups read a confidentiality statement at the beginning of meetings: "What you hear here, stays here. Who you see here, stays here. Please respect the anonymity and confidentiality of all members."

Post Visual Reminders

Hang signs in your meeting space: "Anonymity and confidentiality are the foundation of our safety." Visual cues reinforce the principle.

Address Breaches Promptly

If you learn that someone gossiped about another member or shared details outside the meeting, address it immediately and lovingly: "We need to maintain confidentiality to keep everyone safe. Please don't share others' stories."

Educate Newcomers

Include confidentiality in newcomer orientations. Explain: "You can share your own story however you want, but you may never share someone else's story or presence here without their permission."

"Breaking another person's anonymity is a violation of their trust and our sacred responsibility as members. If we cannot trust that our presence and our shares will remain confidential, we cannot be honest. And without honesty, there is no recovery."

— "Safety and AA: Our Common Welfare," AA bulletin

Common Digital Anonymity Breaks (And How to Avoid Them)

❌ Break 1: Facebook Anniversary Posts ▼

The break: "Today I'm celebrating 5 years sober in Alcoholics Anonymous! My name is Jane Smith and I'm so grateful for this program. #AA #Recovery #Sober"

Why it's a break: Full name + AA identification + public platform = Tradition Eleven violation

✅ Better alternative: "Today I'm celebrating 5 years sober thanks to a 12-step program. Grateful beyond words. #Recovery #Sober" (No full name visible on profile, no specific mention of AA)

Even better: Celebrate at your home group meeting, not online.

❌ Break 2: Instagram Meeting Selfies ▼

The break: Taking a group selfie at your home group meeting and posting it to Instagram with faces visible and caption: "My AA family! Love these people ❤️ #AAMeeting #Recovery"

Why it's a break: You've outed everyone in the photo. Even if they consented to the photo, posting it publicly violates their anonymity because others outside the meeting can now see who attends.

✅ Better alternative: Don't photograph meetings at all. If your group wants a commemorative photo, take it with permission and keep it PRIVATE (not posted online).

❌ Break 3: Location Check-Ins ▼

The break: Checking in on Facebook at "Central AA Club" or tagging your location as "Monday Night AA Meeting"

Why it's a break: You're publicly announcing your AA attendance. Friends scrolling past see: "John Doe is at Central AA Club." This breaks your own anonymity and may inadvertently out others (e.g., if people know your friends who also go there).

✅ Better alternative: Don't use location check-ins at meetings or AA facilities.

❌ Break 4: Zoom Meeting Screenshots ▼

The break: Taking a screenshot of a Zoom meeting showing the participant grid (with names and faces) and posting it on social media: "Great meeting tonight with my online AA group!"

Why it's a break: You've outed every person in that screenshot without their consent. Even if the Zoom meeting was password-protected, the screenshot is now public.

✅ Better alternative: Never screenshot or record Zoom meetings. If your group wants to document attendance for records, use a sign-in sheet, not screenshots.

Reference: "Anonymity Online," GSO pamphlet

❌ Break 5: Recovery Blogs/Vlogs Using Full Names ▼

The break: Starting a YouTube channel or blog titled "John Smith's AA Recovery Journey" where you discuss your experience in AA using your full name and face.

Why it's a break: YouTube is public media. Full name + face + AA identification = Tradition Eleven violation.

✅ Better alternative: Use a pseudonym or first name only. Title it "My Recovery Journey" (not "My AA Journey"). Say "I'm in a 12-step program" rather than naming AA specifically. Or focus on recovery generally without tying it to AA.

Note: Many successful recovery influencers do this well by maintaining anonymity while still sharing their story.

❌ Break 6: LinkedIn Professional Endorsements ▼

The break: Listing "Alcoholics Anonymous Volunteer" or "AA Group Secretary" on your LinkedIn profile under Experience or Volunteer Work, with your full professional name and photo.

Why it's a break: LinkedIn is public media tied to your professional identity. Employers, colleagues, clients can see it. This breaks Tradition Eleven.

✅ Better alternative: List "12-Step Fellowship Volunteer" or "Community Support Group Organizer" without naming AA. Or simply don't list it — AA service is its own reward; it doesn't need to be on your resume.

  • Never post photos showing other members' faces without explicit written consent
  • Never tag other members in posts or photos
  • Use first name only in online recovery spaces
  • Be mindful of what's in your photo backgrounds (AA literature, meeting flyers)

  • Read the policy at the beginning of every meeting (especially Zoom meetings)
  • Post signs: "No photos, recordings, or social media posts in this space"
  • For Zoom meetings, disable participant screen-sharing and recording by default
  • If your group has a Facebook page, never post photos of members
  • Address digital anonymity breaks promptly and lovingly
  • Include digital anonymity in newcomer orientations and Traditions discussions

Anonymity & Your Home Group: Where Principles Are Practiced Daily

⏰ 10 minutes

This morning we said that your home group is "where it all begins." It's also where anonymity is learned, practiced, and protected every single week. Your home group is the laboratory where abstract principles become concrete actions.

How Your Home Group Practices Anonymity

Anonymity isn't just about not appearing on TV. It's woven into the fabric of your home group's daily operations. Here's how:

🔄
1. Rotating Service Positions

Why: Rotation ensures no one becomes indispensable or "the face" of the group. It's anonymity in action — no permanent leaders, only rotating trusted servants.

Recommended terms:

  • Group Service Representative (GSR): 2 years
  • Secretary: 1-2 years
  • Treasurer: 1-2 years
  • Greeter/Coffee Maker: 6 months to 1 year

Reference: The A.A. Service Manual, p. S19

⚖️
2. Equal Voice in Group Conscience

Why: In group conscience, everyone's vote is equal regardless of sobriety time, outside status, or how loudly they speak. One person, one vote. This is Tradition Twelve in practice.

How it works: The member with 40 years and the newcomer with 40 days both get one vote. The doctor and the dishwasher have equal say. No hierarchy.

"At the level of Tradition, therefore, we are all equal."

— Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions, p. 184

📚
3. Conference-Approved Literature Only

Why: Using only conference-approved literature (Big Book, Twelve and Twelve, pamphlets, etc.) ensures we're following principles (AA's collective wisdom) rather than personalities (individual interpretations).

What this means: No outside books, personal interpretations, or non-AA materials read during meetings. Personal shares are welcome; replacing AA literature with personal philosophy is not.

Reference: "The A.A. Group" pamphlet, p. 25

🤐
4. First Names Only

Why: Using first names (or even pseudonyms) removes outside status and credentials. We're all just alcoholics trying to stay sober. No "Dr. Smith" or "Professor Jones" — just "Bob" and "Mary."

Exception: Legal or safety reasons (two members with the same first name, or someone uncomfortable using their real name) — but still no last names.

"We meet as equals. Our names, our professions, our circumstances outside these rooms — none of that matters here."

🔇
5. No Crosstalk or Interruptions

Why: No crosstalk ensures that what is shared matters more than who shares it. We listen without rebutting, advising, or debating — placing the principle of listening above our personal reactions.

What it looks like: Everyone gets uninterrupted time to share. No one comments on, critiques, or responds to another's share during the meeting.

🚫
6. No Promotion or Recognition

Why: We don't celebrate individual members publicly in ways that create "stars" or hierarchies. Sobriety anniversaries are acknowledged, but humbly — a coin, not a trophy ceremony.

What to avoid: "Member of the Month" awards, public accolades for service, special seating for old-timers. These violate the spirit of anonymity.

Common Anonymity Breaks & How to Address Them

⏰ 10 minutes

Even with the best intentions, anonymity breaks happen. Most occur out of enthusiasm (excitement to share recovery) or ignorance (not understanding the Traditions) — not malice. Here's how to address them with love and clarity.

The "Loving Correction" Approach

When addressing anonymity breaks, lead with love and education, not shame or punishment. Remember: we're all learning. Even Bill W. broke anonymity multiple times before fully understanding its importance.

The goal is to protect AA, not to humiliate the person. Approach them privately, with compassion, and offer resources (like the "Understanding Anonymity" pamphlet).

Common Scenarios & How to Handle Them

Scenario 1: Member Posts Meeting Photo on Facebook ▼

What happened: Sarah posts a group photo from your home group on Facebook, showing faces and captioning: "Love my Tuesday night AA crew!"

❌ Wrong response: Call her out publicly at the next meeting: "Sarah, you broke anonymity. That's unacceptable."

✅ Right response:

  1. Pull Sarah aside privately after the meeting (or call/text her)
  2. Speak lovingly: "Hey Sarah, I saw your post. I know you were excited to share, but posting photos with faces violates our members' anonymity. Some people may not want their attendance at AA public."
  3. Ask her to take it down: "Would you mind deleting the post? And in the future, no photos at meetings — or if you must take one, keep it private and get everyone's explicit permission first."
  4. Offer resources: "Here's the 'Understanding Anonymity' pamphlet. It explains the difference between personal and public anonymity."

Follow-up: Suggest the group add a technology policy if one doesn't exist.

Scenario 2: Member Discusses Another Member's Relapse Outside Meetings ▼

What happened: John tells several group members (outside the meeting): "Did you hear? Mike relapsed last week. He was at a bar downtown."

❌ Wrong response: Ignore it or participate in the gossip.

✅ Right response:

  1. Speak to John privately: "John, I understand you're concerned about Mike, but sharing his relapse with others is a breach of confidentiality."
  2. Educate: "If you're worried about Mike, reach out to him directly or speak to his sponsor — but don't discuss his situation with others. That's gossip, not support."
  3. Remind: "What happens outside meetings stays outside meetings, just like what happens inside meetings stays inside meetings."

If John's gossip continues: Bring it to the group conscience. Repeated breaches of confidentiality threaten the group's safety.

Scenario 3: Newcomer Doesn't Understand Anonymity ▼

What happened: A newcomer with 2 weeks sober posts on Instagram: "Just left my AA meeting! Feeling hopeful. My name is Alex Johnson and I'm an alcoholic. #AA #Recovery"

❌ Wrong response: Public shaming or ignoring the break.

✅ Right response:

  1. Reach out gently (sponsor, if they have one, should do this): "Hey Alex, I'm so glad you're feeling hopeful! I wanted to share something about AA that might help you going forward."
  2. Educate: "In AA, we practice anonymity at the public level — which includes social media. You're welcome to post about your recovery, but we suggest not using your full name and 'AA' together in public posts. It protects you and the fellowship."
  3. Explain why: "If you relapse (which we hope you don't!), you won't want your recovery journey tied to your name online forever. And it protects AA from being judged by individual relapses."
  4. Offer an alternative: "You could edit the post to say 'Just left my recovery meeting' or 'Working a 12-step program' instead of naming AA."

Remember: Newcomers often don't know better. This is a teaching moment, not a punishment.

Scenario 4: Old-Timer Dominates Meetings (Personality Over Principle) ▼

What happened: Bob has 35 years of sobriety and is beloved. However, he regularly speaks for 10-15 minutes, interrupts others, and gives unsolicited advice during shares. Newer members feel intimidated.

❌ Wrong response: Let it continue because "Bob's a great guy and he's earned the right to talk."

✅ Right response:

  1. Speak to Bob privately first: "Bob, we love having you in the group. Your experience is invaluable. However, we've noticed that shares sometimes run long, and newer members aren't getting a chance to speak. Would you be willing to keep your shares to 3-5 minutes so everyone gets a turn?"
  2. If Bob resists, remind him of Tradition Twelve: "We place principles before personalities. The principle here is that everyone deserves equal time to share. Even with 35 years, your voice isn't more important than the newcomer's."
  3. If the behavior continues, address it at a group conscience meeting: "We need to enforce a 5-minute sharing limit for everyone to ensure equal participation."

Key point: Seniority doesn't grant privilege. Principles win.

Scenario 5: Someone Is Secretly Recording Zoom Meetings ▼

What happened: You discover that a member has been recording Zoom meetings without the group's knowledge or consent.

❌ Wrong response: Ignore it or handle it informally.

✅ Right response:

  1. Address it immediately and seriously. Recording meetings without consent is a major breach of anonymity and confidentiality.
  2. Speak to the person: "We learned you've been recording meetings. This is a serious violation of our members' anonymity. Why were you doing this?"
  3. Listen to their reason (they may have a legitimate need, like language barriers or hearing difficulty — but that still doesn't excuse secret recording)
  4. Require deletion: "All recordings must be deleted immediately. If you need accommodations, let's discuss alternatives that don't violate anonymity."
  5. If they refuse or if recordings were shared, bring it to group conscience: The group may need to ask them to leave for the safety of other members.

Prevention: State clearly at the beginning of every Zoom meeting: "This meeting is not recorded. Recording or screenshotting is prohibited."

Scenario 6: Local Newspaper Wants to Do a Story on Your Group ▼

What happened: A local reporter contacts your group: "We want to do a human interest story on how AA has helped your community. Can we interview some members and take photos at your meeting?"

❌ Wrong response: Agree immediately because "it's good publicity."

✅ Right response:

  1. Refer them to AA's Public Information office (PI committee) at the district or area level, not your individual group
  2. Explain: "We appreciate your interest, but Alcoholics Anonymous has a public relations policy. We don't promote ourselves in the media, and our members maintain anonymity at the public level."
  3. Offer alternatives: "We can provide you with conference-approved literature about AA, or connect you with our district's PI chair who can speak about AA in general terms without identifying themselves fully."
  4. Do NOT allow them to photograph your meeting or interview members using full names and faces

Reference: "Understanding Anonymity," pamphlet, pp. 13-15

When to Involve Your Group Conscience

Most anonymity breaks can be handled with private, loving conversations. However, bring it to group conscience if:

  • The breaks are repeated after being addressed
  • The breaks are serious (recording meetings, outing others publicly)
  • The person refuses to acknowledge the break or change behavior
  • Multiple members have been affected
  • The group needs to establish a new policy (e.g., technology policy) to prevent future breaks

"We approach anonymity breaks with the same spirit we approach relapse: with compassion, not judgment. We educate, we love, we set boundaries when necessary. But we never shame. Shame is not the AA way."

— Adapted from AA Principles

  • Has your home group dealt with social media anonymity breaks? How did you handle them?
  • Do you think Zoom meetings make it harder to maintain anonymity? Why or why not?
  • How do you navigate posting about recovery on social media while respecting anonymity?
3. Attraction vs. Promotion ▼
  • How does your home group practice "attraction rather than promotion"?
  • Have you ever felt the tension between wanting to let people know AA exists and respecting anonymity?
  • What's the difference between "attraction" and "hiding"? Where's the line?
4. Principles Before Personalities ▼
  • Can you share an example of when your home group had to "place principles before personalities"?
  • Have you ever been the "personality" that needed to be checked? How did that feel?
  • How do we balance honoring old-timers' experience with ensuring they don't dominate?
5. Confidentiality in Practice ▼
  • Have you ever seen someone at a meeting and wondered whether to acknowledge them outside the room?
  • What do you do when you see a fellow AA member "in the wild" (grocery store, workplace, etc.)?
  • How do you navigate confidentiality when your spouse or close friends want to know "how was the meeting?"
6. Celebrity Anonymity ▼
  • What do you think when a celebrity publicly discloses their AA membership?
  • Do you think it helps reduce stigma, or does it violate Tradition Eleven?

  • Does your home group have a technology/social media policy? If so, how's it working?

  • What anonymity situations have you encountered that felt like "gray areas" without clear answers?

9. Anonymity & Sponsorship ▼
  • How do you maintain confidentiality with your sponsees?
  • Do you ever discuss your sponsees with your own sponsor? Where's the line?
  • How do you model anonymity for those you sponsor?
10. Looking Forward ▼
  • Based on tonight's discussion, what's one thing you'll do differently regarding anonymity?
  • What's one action step your home group could take to strengthen anonymity practices?
  • How will you carry tonight's message forward?
