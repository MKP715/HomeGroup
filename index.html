Section 2

Historical Evolution

⏰ 15 minutes

From living fellowship to structured home groups — the term "home group" does not appear in AA's earliest literature

1935 – 1939
The Smith Home Era — The Prototype

Dr. Bob and Anne Smith's residence at 855 Ardmore Avenue, Akron functioned as meeting house, laboratory of recovery, and refuge. Bill W. lived there several months. Meetings included "quiet time, prayer, and bible readings" with Anne conducting morning readings from the Book of James. Members hospitalized prospects before allowing them to join.

Dr. Bob and the Good Oldtimers, p. 101

May 1939
First Group Named "Alcoholics Anonymous"

Cleveland, Ohio. Clarence S. wrote to Bill W. describing their approach: "Not too much emphasis on spiritual business at meetings. Have discussions after meetings of any business or questions arising. Plenty of fellowship all the time."

Dr. Bob and the Good Oldtimers, p. 167

1940
The Akron Manual — Earliest "Home Group" Language

Written and distributed by "Dr. Bob's Home Group, Akron AA group #1." It stated: "Meetings provide a means for an exchange of ideas, the renewing of fellowships... a sense of security" and "Remember that attendance at meetings is one of the most important requisites of remaining sober."

1946 – 1950
The Twelve Traditions Developed

Bill W. wrote the Traditions (first published in the Grapevine, April 1946), codifying decades of group experience. These became the spiritual principles governing how groups — including home groups — relate to each other and to AA as a whole.

Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions (1953)

1993
First Grapevine Book: "The Home Group"

The concept formally crystallized with this publication collecting member stories about their home group experiences. This marked the first dedicated AA publication on the topic.

September 2000
"The Home Group" Becomes Grapevine Department

Due to overwhelming reader response, "The Home Group" became a permanent dedicated department in AA Grapevine magazine.

Present Day
30th Anniversary Edition — Including Virtual Meetings

"The Home Group: Heartbeat of AA" now includes content on virtual meetings, demonstrating the concept continues to evolve while its core purpose remains unchanged.

Grapevine publication (not pamphlet P-88 as sometimes misidentified)

Discussion Prompt
"The term 'home group' emerged organically from decades of practice, not from a rule book. What does this tell us about how AA works?"

The difference between visiting a place and making it home

"A.A. groups generally continue to exist outside the prescribed meeting hours, ready to provide Twelfth Step help when needed."

— P-16 "The A.A. Group," pages 12–13

Membership Is Self-Declared

"Just as we are members of A.A. if we say we are, so are we members of a group if we say we are."

Three Elements That Set the Home Group Apart

👥
1. Accountability & Expectation
"Part of my commitment is to show up at my home group meetings, greet newcomers at the door, and be available to them... My fellow group members are the people who know me, listen to me, and steer me straight when I am off in left field."

"A.A.'s take turns doing the services needed for group meetings and other functions... within the A.A. group, these services are easy to do, and they do wonders for us. They build a sturdy backbone for our recovery."

✅
3. Voting & Group Conscience
"With membership comes the right to vote on issues that might affect the group and might also affect A.A. as a whole — a process that forms the very cornerstone of A.A.'s service structure."

Spiritual Benefits

The Home Group & Sponsorship Connection

⏰ 10 minutes

A symbiotic relationship — each strengthens the other

The pamphlet "Questions and Answers on Sponsorship" (P-15) explicitly connects home group and sponsorship, describing sponsorship as "the bridge enabling the new person to meet other alcoholics — in a home group and other groups visited."

🎯
Guiding Newcomers
P-15 states a sponsor:
  • "Encourages, and if necessary, helps the newcomer to attend a variety of A.A. meetings"
  • "Introduces the newcomer to other members"
  • "Urges the newcomer to join in group activities as soon as possible"

P-15, pp. 7–8, 12

🔧
Service Sponsorship
P-15 recommends: "The service sponsor begins by encouraging the member to become active in their home group — coffee, literature, cleanup, attending business or intergroup meetings, etc."

Service sponsors should: "Be familiar with the AA Service Manual, Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions, Circles of Love and Service, and The AA Home Group pamphlet."

P-15, pp. 25–26

Building Sponsorship Programs Within the Group

"Active sponsorship programs within a group remind all members of the group's primary purpose. They serve to unite a group and keep it mindful of First Things First."

— P-15, pp. 23–25

Suggested practices: regular beginners meetings, member assignments to greet newcomers, and announcements offering temporary sponsors.

"...he finds he cannot keep this priceless gift unless he carries the A.A. message... the moment this Twelfth Step work forms a group, another discovery is made — that most individuals cannot recover unless there is a group."

— Twelve Steps and Twelve Traditions, p. 130

Real-World Scenarios

🎭 Scenario: The Newcomer at the Door
Someone walks into your home group for the very first time. They're shaking. They don't know anyone. They sit in the back. The meeting starts. The meeting ends. Nobody talks to them. They leave. They never come back.

Now picture the alternative: A greeter meets them. Introduces them to three people. Hands them a phone list. After the meeting, two members invite them for coffee. One offers to be a temporary sponsor.

Which scenario reflects our primary purpose?
📚 Scenario: The Service Sponsor
A member with 3 years of sobriety has worked all 12 Steps but has never held a service position beyond coffee maker. Their Step sponsor suggests finding a "service sponsor" — someone to guide them through the Traditions, the Concepts, and how to be a trusted servant.

P-15 says service sponsorship begins in the home group with simple things: coffee, literature, cleanup, attending business meetings.

Does your home group encourage service sponsorship?

Section 8

The Twelve Traditions as Home Group Guidance

⏰ 15 minutes

Five Traditions that most directly shape home group life — click each to expand

1 Unity — Common Welfare Comes First

"Each member of Alcoholics Anonymous is but a small part of a great whole. A.A. must continue to live or most of us will surely die. Hence our common welfare comes first. But individual welfare follows close afterward."

— Tradition One (Long Form), 12&12, p. 189

The 12&12 elaborates (p. 129): "Without unity, the heart of A.A. would cease to beat; our world arteries would no longer carry the life-giving grace of God; His gift to us would be spent aimlessly."

2 Group Conscience — One Ultimate Authority

"For our group purpose there is but one ultimate authority — a loving God as He may express Himself in our group conscience. Our leaders are but trusted servants; they do not govern."

— Tradition Two

Bill W. explained in AA Comes of Age (p. 99): "Harder still to accept was the now proven fact that the conscience of the group, when properly informed of the facts and issues and principles involved, was often wiser than any leader, self-appointed or not."

3 Membership — The Only Requirement

"The only requirement for A.A. membership is a desire to stop drinking."

— Tradition Three

The long form adds (12&12, p. 139): "Any two or three alcoholics gathered together for sobriety may call themselves an A.A. group, provided that, as a group, they have no other affiliation."

This means: "You are an A.A. member if you say so. You can declare yourself in; nobody can keep you out."

4 Autonomy — Each Group Is Self-Governing

"Each group should be autonomous except in matters affecting other groups or A.A. as a whole."

— Tradition Four

This allows each home group to develop its own character — meeting format, literature used, procedures followed — while maintaining connection to AA's principles. No two home groups are alike, and that is by design.

5 Primary Purpose — Carry the Message

"Each group has but one primary purpose — to carry its message to the alcoholic who still suffers."

— Tradition Five, 12&12, p. 150

The 12&12 explains: "'Shoemaker, stick to thy last!'...better do one thing supremely well than many badly. That is the central theme of this Tradition."

Section 9

Participating in Informed Group Conscience

⏰ 10 minutes

The process that distinguishes AA from other organizations

"The 'group conscience' is the collective conscience of the group membership and thus represents substantial unanimity on an issue before definitive action is taken. This is achieved by the group members through the sharing of full information, individual points of view, and the practice of A.A. principles. To be fully 'informed' requires a willingness to listen to minority opinions with an open mind."

— P-16 "The A.A. Group," pp. 28–30

Essential Elements of the Process

🔍 Information Gathering ▼

All pertinent facts are presented before discussion begins. This may include reading relevant Traditions, reviewing precedents from other groups, or consulting Conference-approved literature.

💬 Full Discussion ▼

All views are heard, including minority opinions. Every home group member who wishes to speak has the opportunity. No voice is silenced.

⏳ Deliberation Time ▼

On sensitive issues, groups work slowly. There is no rush. Some groups table important issues for a month to allow reflection and prayer.

⚖ Avoiding Dominant Voices ▼

Membership remains wary of dominating personalities. Tradition Two reminds us that leaders are trusted servants — they do not govern.

✅ Substantial Unanimity ▼

Results rest on more than a simple "yes" or "no" count. Substantial unanimity (typically two-thirds) ensures that the group truly speaks with one voice.

When Even a Co-Founder Was Overruled

"'Bill, haven't you often said right here in this meeting that sometimes the good is the enemy of the best? Well, this is a plain case of it. You can't do this thing to us!' So spoke the group conscience. The group was right and I was wrong... Here was the true voice, welling up out of my friends. I listened and — thank God — I obeyed."

— Bill W., AA Comes of Age, p. 101

Business meetings typically occur monthly or quarterly, with voting restricted to those who identify the group as their home group. Each member has one vote — the foundation of AA's democratic character.

Practice Scenarios — What Would Your Group Do?

🎭 The Controversial Proposal
At the business meeting, a member proposes allowing non-Conference-approved literature to be read during meetings. Several members support it; several strongly oppose. Walk through how an informed group conscience would handle this. What information would you gather? What Traditions apply?
🎭 The Dominant Personality
One member with 20 years of sobriety speaks the longest at every business meeting. When they share their opinion, newer members go quiet and defer. Motions tend to go whichever way this person votes. How does your group address this while honoring Tradition Two?

Choosing a Home Group Wisely

⏰ 8 minutes

Practical guidance from AA literature

Living Sober advises newcomers to try different meetings before settling (Chapter 29, pp. 75–81).

📍
Practical Considerations
  • Meeting time and location for consistent attendance
  • Format that resonates (speaker, discussion, step study, Big Book)
  • Size that feels comfortable
  • Service positions appropriate to sobriety
🤝
Fellowship Considerations
  • Members whose recovery you admire
  • Presence of potential sponsors
  • Welcoming atmosphere toward newcomers
  • Diversity of sobriety lengths
🌐
Service Considerations
  • Active GSR attending district/area
  • Business meetings held regularly
  • Room to grow into responsibility
  • Connected to intergroup & broader AA

"The home group is where recovery begins; it is where AA members grow up in sobriety by the time-honored process of trial and error, to discover that they can be loved, warts and all. It is where they learn to put the needs of others, especially the needs of the group, ahead of their own desires."

— The Home Group: Heartbeat of AA (Grapevine), Foreword

Section 11

Common Challenges & How Groups Address Them

⏰ 10 minutes

Every home group faces recurring tests of the Traditions

🗣
Dominant Personalities
Threatens Tradition Two's servant leadership. The 12&12 teaches: group conscience, properly informed, proves wiser than any individual.

Solution: Ensure all voices are heard. Rotate leadership positions. Practice "principles before personalities."

Tradition 2

🔒
Cliques & Exclusivity
Contradicts Tradition Three's open membership. P-16 reminds: "We also learn firsthand, through the group's workings, how to place 'principles before personalities' in the interest of carrying the A.A. message."

Solution: Assign greeters. Consciously include newcomers in fellowship.

Tradition 3

📰
Outside Issues
Violates Traditions Six (no endorsement) and Ten (no opinion on outside issues). Groups must maintain focus on alcoholism recovery.

Solution: Gently redirect. Read Tradition Ten at business meetings. Stay focused on primary purpose.

Traditions 6 & 10

💰
Financial Challenges
Tests Tradition Seven (self-supporting through own contributions). Transparency and education are essential.

Solution: Regular financial reports. Maintain prudent reserves. Educate members on service structure funding needs.

Tradition 7

😴
Apathy in Service
Threatens group sustainability when few members participate. Groups address this by demonstrating how service strengthens recovery and creating welcoming entry points for new service commitment. Even making coffee is spiritually significant.

The AA Service Triangle

Closing Reflection

The home group is where we learn to give it away — one meeting, one service commitment, one newcomer welcomed, one vote cast in group conscience, one day at a time.

For the member new to recovery, the home group offers a place to belong after years of isolation.
For the member growing in service, it provides the foundation for contribution to AA's mission.
For the long-timer, it remains the place where fundamentals are practiced and newcomers welcomed.

At every stage of recovery, the home group answers AA's central paradox: "It is the great paradox of A.A. that we know we can seldom keep the precious gift of sobriety unless we give it away." (12&12, p. 151)

Our Local Legacy
1943 — Esther E., whose story "A Flower of the South" appears in the Big Book (2nd & 3rd editions), brought AA to Dallas. She started the first group in her living room after finding no active AA when she arrived from Houston. Her April 5, 1943 letter to New York: "The new Dallas Group met for their first time last night!"

1945 — First meeting place outside a home: 912½ Main Street, downtown Dallas. Fewer than 20 members sober by year's end.

Sept 18, 1947 — Dallas Central Office opened on Akard Street in the Davis Building. Dick P. was the first director.

Today — NETA Area 65 encompasses 29 districts and 512+ groups across North East Texas. District 22 is part of that legacy — and every home group in our district carries forward what Esther started in her living room 80+ years ago.

Esther's inscribed Big Book, signed by Bill W., is on display at the Dallas Central Office.

Scenario Bank for Discussion

⏰ Use as needed for roundtables

Real-world situations for group discussion — click each to expand

🎭 The Relapsing Member ▼

A home group member with 2 years of sobriety relapses. They come back to the meeting, visibly ashamed. Some members welcome them warmly. Others seem uncomfortable. One old-timer says at the business meeting, "Maybe we need to be more careful about who we let share."

Discussion: What Traditions apply? (3, 1, 5) What is the group's responsibility? What would your home group do?

🎭 The Non-Alcoholic Attendee ▼

Someone attends your open home group regularly. They share that they're not an alcoholic but have a family member who is. They want to "join" the group. At the business meeting, a member asks whether this person should be allowed to vote.

Discussion: What does Tradition Three say? What's the difference between an open and closed meeting? Can this person be a "member"?

🎭 The Money Question ▼

Your home group has accumulated $3,000 in its treasury. Some members want to donate it all to intergroup. Others say keep a larger prudent reserve. The treasurer suggests splitting it between the district, NETA 65, and GSO.

Discussion: What does Tradition Seven say? What does the AA Service Manual suggest about fund flow? What is a "prudent reserve"? How does your group currently handle excess funds?

🎭 The Format War ▼

Half the home group wants to switch from a speaker meeting to a Big Book study. The other half loves the current format. The debate has been going on for three business meetings. Attendance is dropping because people are frustrated.

Discussion: How does an informed group conscience handle this? What does Tradition Four allow? Could the group compromise? What if the group splits — is that failure or growth?

🎭 The Online Question ▼

Since the pandemic, your home group has offered a hybrid option (in-person + Zoom). Some members think Zoom should be permanent. Others feel it reduces the quality of fellowship and that "real AA" is in person only.

Discussion: What does Tradition Four say about group autonomy? How do you balance accessibility with the value of in-person fellowship? What does the 30th Anniversary "Home Group: Heartbeat" say about virtual meetings?

🎭 The Anonymity Break ▼

A home group member posts a photo from the meeting on social media, tagging other members by name. Some members are upset. Others say, "It's just social media, relax."

Discussion: What do Traditions 11 and 12 say about anonymity? What is the difference between personal anonymity and breaking others' anonymity? How should the group address this?

🎭 The Crosstalk Debate ▼

Your home group has always had a "no crosstalk" guideline. A newer member says this feels cold and clinical — they want people to respond to each other, ask follow-up questions, and have real dialogue. An old-timer says the no-crosstalk rule protects vulnerable members from unsolicited advice.

Discussion: Is "no crosstalk" a Tradition or a group guideline? How does Tradition Four apply? How do you balance safety with genuine connection?

🎭 The Dying Group ▼

A home group that once had 30 regular members now averages 6. The remaining members are all old-timers. No newcomers have attended in months. The lease on the meeting space is expensive. Two members suggest closing the group.

Discussion: What can a group do to revitalize? Should groups ever close? What is the group's responsibility to the alcoholic who might walk in next week? How does Tradition Five guide this decision?

History • Part 1

Larry J.: The Man Who Carried AA to Texas with Only a Book

⏰ 10 minutes

Houston, 1940 — AA arrives in Texas through desperation, not planning

📖
The Desperate Messenger
Found in freezing Cleveland weather. One lung collapsed from TB. Tube protruding from his chest. "Drunk himself into the gutter." Louis Seltzer, editor of the Cleveland Press, sent a search party.

Sponsors: Dr. Bob Smith & Clarence Snyder

🚅
Spiritual Awakening on a Train
He had never attended a single AA meeting. His sponsors gave him a Big Book the night before he left for Texas. On the train to Houston, reading the book, Larry experienced a spiritual awakening — "all in one piece again."

Arrived Houston: January 1940

What Larry J. Accomplished in Texas

Mar 15, 1940 First Houston Meeting (YWCA)
Feb 8, 1940 First AA Pamphlet Ever
Apr 1, 1940 "The Texas Prayer" Written
💡 Did You Know?
The Houston Press articles Larry arranged became the first AA pamphlet ever published — the only AA literature besides the Big Book until late 1940. By September 1940, Houston was listed among only 9 "secure" AA groups in the entire United States, alongside Akron, New York, Cleveland, Detroit, Chicago, Los Angeles, Philadelphia, and Washington D.C.

"Story of a 'Way Out' for Hopeless Drinkers: How an Idea Originated by Ex-Alcoholics Has Helped 2000 to Recover."

— Headline from Houston Press articles, February 1940

⚠ The Cautionary Lesson
Larry ran the Houston group with "something of an iron hand." When the group voted for a steering committee based on Eastern AA practices, Larry pulled out resentfully and relapsed. He returned in 1943 but died later that year. His legacy endured: the people he helped included Esther E., who would carry the message to Dallas.
History • Part 2

The False Start: Kent W. & the Lessons of Promotion

⏰ 10 minutes

Dallas, 1941–1942 — Why publicity alone cannot sustain a spiritual fellowship

📨
Ruth T.'s Cry for Help — June 14, 1941
The earliest recorded impulse for recovery in Dallas: Ruth T. sent a letter to Ruth Hock, Bill Wilson's secretary in New York, inquiring about AA in her area. A lone woman desperately seeking connection. Demand for recovery existed in Dallas before any supply of meetings was available. Remember her name — she'll appear again.

"Chief Sponsor" Arrives — December 1, 1941

Kent W. arrived in Dallas with sobriety from Florida, a public relations background, and a title he gave himself: "Chief Sponsor" of Dallas AA — a title that directly contradicts the spirit of rotating leadership and humility later codified in Tradition Two.

— AA History Presentation & Script archives

❌ What Kent W. Did

Venue: White Plaza Hotel — public, commercial, impersonal

Method: Newspaper publicity in the Dallas Morning News

Style: "Chief Sponsor" — hierarchical, personality-driven

Result: 12 attendees, 72 inquiries from the newspaper... then complete collapse.

Kent W. was "never heard from again."

✅ What Would Work (Preview)

Venue: A living room — intimate, domestic, safe

Method: Word of mouth, doctor referrals, personal contact

Style: Service, humility, attraction not promotion

Result: Slow but sustainable growth — still alive 80+ years later

This is what Esther E. would do next year.

History • Part 3

Esther E.: A Flower of the South

⏰ 15 minutes

The true foundation of Dallas AA — April 2, 1943

🌻 "A Flower of the South"
Esther E.'s story appears in the Second and Third Editions of the Big Book (pp. 384–392, 3rd Ed.). She is pictured with AA co-founder Bill Wilson. Her inscribed Big Book remains at the Dallas Central Office to this day — a physical link between the founders and our local fellowship.

The Journey to Sobriety

New Orleans — Early Life
Social Drinking Was Normal

Raised where wine with dinner and cordials afterward were standard. At her own wedding, seeing her nervousness, her father ordered a servant to bring her something to drink. The servant brought a water glass full of bourbon.

1927–1932 — First Time in Dallas
"All the Doctors, Hospitals, and Cures"

Her drinking progressed severely. She tried every treatment available — "the Sanitarium three times." Eventually moved to Houston where her drinking continued to escalate.

March 1, 1941
📰 The Saturday Evening Post Changes Everything

Esther's husband read the Jack Alexander article — the article that transformed AA from 2,000 members to 8,000 in one year.

"If you will try this thing, I'll go along with you. If you don't, you will have to go home. I cannot sit by and watch you destroy yourself!"

— Esther E.'s husband's ultimatum

May 16, 1941
🌟 Esther E.'s Sobriety Date

Got sober in Houston after writing to the General Service Office in New York. Within a week she received AA literature and a hand-written letter from Ruth Hock. "That personal touch did a lot to help."

March 29, 1943
Esther Writes to New York from Dallas

"This is where I had been so sick for five years. Where I started trying out all the doctors, hospitals and cures (the Sanitarium three times) so I've lots to do. First off, four doctors to call on and let them look over 'exhibit A' (me)! My minister (Episcopal) has two prospects for us."

— Esther E.'s letter to General Service Office

April 2, 1943
🌟 THE FOUNDING OF DALLAS AA

The first meeting of "The Dallas Group" in Esther's home. Ruth T. was present — the woman who had written the initial inquiry letter in 1941. The seeker and the carrier of the message finally united.

This date marks the lineage point for EVERY AA group in the Dallas metroplex today.

Tues & Fri Meeting Days
8:00 PM Meeting Time
< 20 Sober by 1945
19+ yrs Esther's Sobriety
💡 The Contrast That Teaches
Kent W.'s hotel ballroom attracted curiosity. Esther's living room fostered intimacy. Kent called himself "Chief Sponsor." Esther simply said she had "lots to do." Kent used newspaper publicity. Esther visited doctors and her minister. The home group concept didn't begin in a conference room — it began in a home.
History • Part 4

Post-War Expansion: Searcy W. & the Suburban Shift

⏰ 10 minutes

1946–1947 — Decentralizing AA from downtown to where people lived

💪
Searcy W. (Whaley) — Dallas's Elder Statesman
First heard about AA in 1945 from an old drinking buddy. Lost his job in November 1945, stayed drunk until April 1946. Placed in a "drying out place" off Maple Street. On his third day, taken to a meeting at 912½ Main Street.

Sobriety date: May 5, 1946

57 years of continuous sobriety until death in September 2003

🏠
The Suburban Group — September 1946
Founded at the corner of Dickason and Sale Streets, near Lee Park, just north of downtown. Just four months after getting sober, Searcy helped create the prototype for the neighborhood group.

✅ First decentralization of AA from downtown to neighborhoods

What the Suburban Group Birthed

The Suburban Group became the mother of many Dallas groups. Out of it grew:

Preston Group Belmont Group Belwood Group Central Group Town North Group Oak Cliff Group (same era) Many others through subsequent decades
🤔 Quick Question
"Does anyone here know which group YOUR home group descended from? Every group has a genealogy — and it may trace back to Esther's living room or the Suburban Group."

The Dallas Central Office Opens — September 18, 1947

📞
A Musty Old Office, Filled with AA Caring
Davis Building on Akard Street. With only two active groups (The Dallas Group & the Suburban Group), local leadership established service infrastructure. "An act of profound faith."
🙌
Dick P. — First Office Manager
Suffered from "Jamaica Ginger Paralysis" (Jake Leg) — a permanent disability caused by toxic Prohibition-era alcohol substitutes. Despite his physical limitations, he managed the phones, coordinated Twelfth Step calls, and sold literature. A living embodiment of the Responsibility Statement.

History • Part 5

Ebby Thacher: Bill's Sponsor Saved in Dallas

⏰ 10 minutes

The most remarkable chapter in Dallas AA history — and a lesson about never giving up

✍ First: Searcy W. and the Twelve Traditions

In 1948, 25 AA members from across Texas met in Lubbock. Searcy arranged for Bill Wilson to attend from Phoenix. On the airplane, Bill showed Searcy his handwritten notes — the original Twelve Traditions.

Searcy's initial response was dismissive:

"We don't need this down here. We love each other."

— Searcy W. to Bill Wilson, 1948

At the 1950 International Convention in Cleveland (8,000–9,000 people), the Twelve Traditions were adopted with no dissenting votes. Searcy came to understand they weren't restrictions but protections — guidelines to let AA survive beyond any individual personality.

The Man Who Started It All — Needed Dallas to Save Him

💡
Who Was Ebby Thacher?

Ebby first carried the message to Bill Wilson in November 1934. Without Ebby, there is no Bill W. Without Bill W., there is no AA.

But Ebby struggled with sobriety for 18 years after helping Bill get sober.

💬
Bill Wilson's Request (1953)

When Bill visited Dallas and Searcy asked what he would most like to see happen in AA, Bill replied:

"I'd rather see Ebby have a chance to get sober."

1953
Ebby Arrives in Dallas

Mutual friends found Ebby on the Bowery in New York City. Dried him out briefly. Sent him to Dallas with a pint of whiskey. He arrived at Searcy's clinic "cussing out Bill and Dr. Bob and me and everybody else."

1953–1954
🌟 Ebby Gets Sober at the Suburban Club

Eventually, he asked to attend a meeting. He got sober at the Suburban Club — the same fellowship that had grown from his original message to Bill Wilson could later save him.

1954–1958
Sober in Dallas

After a brief slip in 1954, Ebby met Chloe K. and maintained seven years of sobriety. He left Dallas around 1958–1961.

March 21, 1966
Ebby Thacher Dies

Died with 2½ years of sobriety. His story demonstrates that recovery is possible even after decades of struggle.

Fort Worth, NETA 65 & the Bigger Picture

⏰ 10 minutes

A dual-hub system emerges — and Dallas AA connects to the worldwide fellowship

Fort Worth's Parallel History

📜
George McL. Writes to New York
June 4, 1941 — AA's New York office answered his letter, telling him the only Texas group was in Houston. On July 23, 1941, Fort Worth held its first meeting. George wrote back: "I am happy to report that I have the A.A. well on its way in Fort Worth. At our meeting last night we had seven members."
🚃
Ralph — The Zealous 12th-Stepper
Continuous AA activity in Fort Worth: August 28, 1943. Ralph, a railroad switchman and former deputy sheriff, became "an apostle for the Fellowship, one of the most zealous Twelfth-Steppers of all time." He and George met weekly at George's home, reading the Big Book, talking, meditating, and praying.

The Organizational Evolution

1947
Dallas Central Office Opens (Akard St.)

With only 2 groups. An act of faith in the future.

1968
Central Office Becomes Intergroup

A local member loaned $2,000 to buy literature. 8–9 groups now operating.

1977
Fort Worth Central Office Established

After two previous failed attempts. Western metroplex infrastructure complete.

1989
Dallas Intergroup Association Incorporated

501(c)(3) non-profit status. Formalizing decades of service.

2000
Renamed: Dallas AA Central Office

Current name. Operates aadallas.org, 24/7 hotline (214-887-6699), meeting schedules.

Two Types of Service — One Fellowship

Intergroup / Central Office
Carrying the message (Twelfth Step): printing meeting schedules, maintaining the website, operating the 24/7 hotline, and serving as the "front door" for newcomers.

Dallas AA Fun Facts & Trivia

⏰ 10 minutes

How well do you know your local AA history? Click each to reveal!

🤔 What was the FIRST AA pamphlet ever published? ▼

Larry J.'s Houston Press articles (February 1940) — six anonymous articles that became the only AA literature besides the Big Book until late 1940. AA's first pamphlet was born in Texas!

🤔 What year was Dallas AA founded — and by whom? ▼

April 2, 1943 — by Esther E. in her living room. Her Big Book story is "A Flower of the South" (Big Book, 3rd Edition, pp. 384–392).

🤔 Who is the "Chief Sponsor" and what happened to him? ▼

Kent W. arrived December 1, 1941 and called himself "Chief Sponsor." His hotel-based, publicity-driven effort at the White Plaza Hotel collapsed entirely. He was "never heard from again." A cautionary tale about promotion vs. attraction.

🤔 How many people were sober in Dallas by 1945? ▼

Fewer than twenty! Today there are 160+ groups and thousands of members. Slow, steady growth through attraction, not promotion.

🤔 What is "Jake Leg" and who at Dallas Central Office had it? ▼

"Jamaica Ginger Paralysis" — a permanent disability caused by toxic Prohibition-era alcohol substitutes. Dick P., the first Dallas Central Office manager, suffered from it. Despite his disability, he managed phones and coordinated Twelfth Step calls.

🤔 Who told Bill Wilson that Texas didn't need the Traditions? ▼

Searcy W. in 1948: "We don't need this down here. We love each other." Bill showed him the handwritten Traditions on an airplane. Searcy later came to understand they weren't restrictions but protections.

🤔 Who was the man that first carried the message to Bill Wilson — and where did he later get sober? ▼

Ebby Thacher — he first carried the message to Bill W. in November 1934. After struggling with sobriety for 18 years, he got sober at the Suburban Club in Dallas in 1953. The same fellowship that grew from his original message saved him.

🤔 What article quadrupled AA membership — and directly led to Dallas AA's founding? ▼

The Jack Alexander article in the Saturday Evening Post (March 1, 1941). It grew AA from 2,000 to 8,000 members in one year. Esther E.'s husband read this article, gave her an ultimatum, and she contacted the General Service Office — leading directly to Dallas AA's founding.
